Cloud-Native-Apps-SF Meetup

Jun 14, 2017 | 3 minutes read
Share this: Twitter Facebook Pinterest Google+

In the past week, I was very lucky to be able to attend the local “Cloud Native Apps SF” Meetup group. They hosted their Meetup at the Bitnami HQ in San Francisco, which provided the event space for the group.

The event was all about Serverless, and was heavily focused on Kubernetes. Mark Stemm, a Senior Software Engineer at Sysdig, started off the evening. Mark’s presentation was about solving the problems of resource allocation and monitoring. Furthermore, he wanted to answer the questions:

  • How do we extract data from the System, JVM, and the Network?

  • How do we make sense of the data?

Sisdig - Kubernetes Monitoring


The problem is that when you have a lot of containers, things get messy that it is very hard to monitor each container separately. Sysdig’s solution to this problem is to come up with a system that will measure the stream of all the system calls on a machine. Furthermore, Sysdig let’s you filter the streams to retrieve the data that is relevant for you. Their SaaS product is based on the Sysdig Opensource Terminal, and logs all the activity of your environment to their servers. Fundamentally, it will give you an overview of what is working, and what’s not working.

Going Serverless with Kubeless


Rick Spencer and Sebastien Goasguen, the Vice President and Senior Directors of Binami, respectively, concluded the meetup by giving a talk about Kubeless - A Serverless framework for Kubernetes. With me being still pretty new with the idea of Serverless, I was very interested to learn more about this trending technology. One interesting way that I heard Sebastien Goasguen explain this concept of Serverless was instead of calling it Serverless, he called it Servicesfull. This sounds like literally the opposite of what Serverless is, right? Well, now with containers and cloud services becoming the standard in software development, there are so many services and containers that you have to stitch together, and that is where Serverless is so useful. The functions that you write on a Serverless platform enable you to design apps by stitching together all of your services.

Another interesting thing about Kubeless is that the runtime is instrumented by Prometheus, and the horizontal Pod Autoscaler from Kubernetes. In that way, when you create a function, Prometheus will count the number of requests, and the Pod Autoscaler will take care of the scaling for you. The whole Serverless movement is really about forgetting about the infrastructure and focusing on application design, and thus really adding value to the user. A final remark - the shift towards application design and away from application infrastructure is happening, and it’s happening fast. Currently, Kubernetes is the most commented project on Github; a fact that really shows you the developer community’s increasing interest in this new way of software development.
